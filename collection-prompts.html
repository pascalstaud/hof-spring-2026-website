Collector Model · SS26

Kinsō — 金装 · Adornment

Salon Silhouette · Cleaned · Coach Doors · Silk Gold & Aizome Blue

The Story

Kinsō (金装) — Japanese for “golden adornment.” This collection lives at the intersection of Japanese botanical textile art and Parisian haute couture. It begins with Aizome — the ancient indigo dyeing tradition where craftsmen work with hands stained deep blue from years of patience. Each dip, each oxidation, deepens the colour toward something living.

The two-tone exterior — Silk Gold above the beltline, Aizome Blue below — borrows from the language of fashion, where contrast creates conversation. Like a Japanese designer who first stepped onto the Paris stage, Kinsō bridges two worlds without compromise. The botanical jacquard woven for this expression carries the patience of observation found in early botanical illustration — every petal, every vein recorded with reverence.

Coach Doors open like the binding of a couture lookbook. Inside, the Kintsugi philosophy makes itself felt — gold accents along seams and joins do not hide the construction, they celebrate it. Gold reveals the soul. The interior moves from warm Silk Gold cream leather in the front cabin to deep Aizome Blue in the rear — a threshold between a Parisian atelier and a Japanese tea room.

Kinsō is not a car with a story. It is a story that became a car.

Target Connoisseur

The cultural collector. Art Basel regulars. Fashion-aware, design-educated. They know the difference between Dries Van Noten and Rick Owens. They don’t buy cars — they collect expressions. Their home features curated objects, not accumulations. Age 35–55. Net worth 5M+. They are moved by narrative, not numbers.

Performance Model · SS26

Tokyo — 東京 · Eastern Capital

Sport Silhouette · Standard Doors · Silver/Orange · Cast Crystal Resin

The Story

Tokyo (東京) — Eastern Capital. This collection draws from Edo Kiriko (江戸切子), Tokyo’s own precision glass-cutting tradition dating to 1834. Edo Kiriko is the art of cutting intricate geometric patterns into crystal glass — light trapped inside solid form. Fire, transparency, and absolute control.

The hero material is cast crystal resin — a transparent amber-orange resin inspired by haute horlogerie watchmaking, where you can embed forms and colours inside solid material, like the crystal cases of high-complication timepieces. Throughout the interior — dashboard accents, door panels, steering wheel inlays, speaker surrounds, headliner details — these crystal resin elements glow like captured amber light, turning the cabin into a lantern from within.

Two interior expressions: Expression I pairs deep amber leather with orange crystal resin accents for a warm, enveloping cockpit. Expression II sets white leather against the same orange crystal resin, creating a gallery-like contrast where every amber element reads as a jewel against snow.

The exterior in silver with orange accents (or inverted: orange with silver accents) speaks the same language of precision and fire. H-pattern embossed seats connect to the geometric tradition of Edo Kiriko’s cut-glass patterns. Carbon fiber accents reinforce the performance character.

Tokyo is light made solid. Precision made visible. The city itself, distilled.

Target Connoisseur

The horological mind. Watch collectors who understand complications. They appreciate the intersection of engineering and beauty. Tech-forward but taste-driven. They own Jacob & Co., Richard Mille, or MB&F — watches as mechanical sculpture. Age 30–50. They see a car as a wearable object, an extension of personal precision.

Expression Model · SS26

Notan — 濃淡 · Light-Dark Harmony

Sport Silhouette · Standard Doors · Two-Tone Exterior · Karbonium Accents

The Story

Notan (濃淡) — the Japanese art principle of light-dark interplay, where dark and light are equally important, and neither dominates. In sumi-e ink wash painting, the blank paper IS the composition as much as the brushstroke. In Notan, what is absent defines what is present.

Every Notan expression is inherently two-tone. The exterior pairs contrasting colours in permanent dialogue: dark blue with lighter blue, orange with green, champagne with black, white cabin over Schwarzwald Green body. The colour compositions are not decorative — they are philosophical. Two forces in balance.

The hero material is Karbonium — a premium carbon fiber that can be dyed and coloured, creating unique coloured carbon patterns. This is not standard black carbon fiber. Karbonium takes the accent colour of each composition: blue carbon, green carbon, copper carbon. It transforms the most technical material into an expressive one.

Inside, white leather provides the canvas — the blank space of the Notan principle. Against this, coloured leather accents (piping, steering wheel wrapping, seat edges) and copper or bronze hardware create the “ink strokes” of the composition. H-pattern appears on decorative trim elements, connecting to the broader HOF language.

Notan is the art of seeing both sides at once. Dark needs light. Light needs dark. Neither is the subject. The conversation is.

Target Connoisseur

The design thinker. Architects, creative directors, gallery owners. They understand negative space and compositional tension. They own furniture by Tadao Ando or John Pawson. They see beauty in duality, not decoration. Age 35–55. They gravitate toward objects that contain an idea, not just a surface.

Essence Model · SS26

Ma — 間 · Negative Space

Salon Silhouette · Cleaned · Standard Doors · All Silver · Bouclé & Metallic Leather

The Story

Ma (間) — the Japanese philosophy of negative space, the interval, the pause that gives meaning. In architecture, Ma is the empty room that feels complete. In music, it is the silence between notes that creates the melody. In design, it is the restraint that takes more courage than addition.

Ma is the most minimal HOF expression. A single composition: all silver exterior, no options, no customisation. One way. The right way. The exterior is a silver monolith — reflective, shifting, alive with the light around it. It takes its colour from the world, not from a paint code.

Inside, reduction meets warmth. The hero material is bouclé woven textile — a high-pile, tactile fabric drawn from the world of luxury furniture and sofa upholstery. It covers seats, dashboard surfaces, door panels — creating a living-room-on-wheels feeling. Warm despite its minimalism, like a perfectly empty room that still feels like home.

Metallic leather — leather that looks and feels like brushed metal but IS leather — bridges the silver exterior with the warm interior. When you touch the dashboard, you expect cold metal. Instead, you find warmth. This surprise is Ma’s quiet rebellion: things are not what they seem, and that is the point.

H-pattern appears in decorative trim elements, the only geometric gesture in an otherwise fluid interior. The palette is sand, cream, olive — the colours of a Zen garden in autumn light.

Ma is the luxury of nothing. The space between things, where meaning lives.

Target Connoisseur

The reductionist. They have edited their life to essentials. Their wardrobe is Jil Sander, The Row, Lemaire. Their home is John Pawson or Claudio Silvestrin. They find more luxury in a single perfect object than in a room full of things. Age 40–60. They have moved beyond accumulation into curation of absence. They understand that the most expensive thing in Tokyo real estate is empty space.

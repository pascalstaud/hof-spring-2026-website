Kinsō

Internal: Kenzo Couture · HOF Salon Silhouette · Coach Doors · Pure Surface

The Story

Kinsō (金装) — Japanese for “golden attire.” In the tradition of Kintsugi, where broken pottery is repaired with gold to celebrate its history, Kinsō adorns the surface to reveal the soul within.

The HOF Kinsō is where Parisian haute couture meets Tokyo precision. It begins not with a car, but with a fabric — a botanical jacquard woven exclusively for this expression, depicting the patience of observation found in early botanical illustration. The same eye that once catalogued every petal of a Japanese chrysanthemum now curates every surface of this G-Class.

The two-tone exterior — cream meeting indigo — borrows from fashion's most enduring principle: that contrast creates conversation. Like a Dior gown where structure meets fluidity, Kinsō divides its silhouette into light and dark, day and night, the seen and the sensed.

Coach Doors open like the pages of a couture lookbook. The interior reveals a two-zone world: cream leather in the front cabin, transitioning to deep indigo in the rear — as if crossing the threshold from a Parisian atelier into a Japanese tea room.

Every detail is placed, not added. The botanical motif appears only on close inspection — a hand-placed embroidery that rewards those who look twice. The wheels, finished in white with a gold ring accent, complete a composition that whispers rather than announces.

Kinsō is not a car with a story. It is a story that became a car.

Target Connoisseur

The cultural collector. Art Basel regulars. Fashion-aware, design-educated. They know the difference between Dries Van Noten and Rick Owens. They don't buy cars — they collect expressions. Their home features curated objects, not accumulations. Age 35–55. Net worth 5M+. They are moved by narrative, not numbers.

Seasonal Model · SS26

Tokyo

東京 · Eastern Capital · HOF Sport Silhouette · Sculpted Surface

The Story

Tokyo (東京) — The eastern capital. A city that gave birth to Edo Kiriko — the art of precision-cut glass, practised since 1834 in the workshops of old Tokyo. Where a master craftsman takes a solid block of crystal and, through patience and geometry, reveals the light that was always inside.

The HOF Tokyo begins with this principle: that the most extraordinary material is light, captured in solid form. Its signature element is cast crystal resin — a translucent, hand-cast compound from the world of haute horlogerie, the same gem-like material found in the most exclusive mechanical timepieces. Not plastic. Not glass. A third material — optically pure, alive with depth, shaped in HOF’s Manufaktur by artisans trained in the same disciplines as Swiss watchcase makers.

Inside the Tokyo, crystal resin flows across the entire cabin — the dashboard accent strip, both door panels, the steering wheel spokes, the bass speaker surround, even the headliner trim. Each piece is hand-poured in deep amber-orange, cured, and polished until it holds light the way a cut gemstone holds fire. The effect is unmistakable: a cabin that glows from within.

The second signature is the H-pattern seat — HOF’s geometric quilting language, a grid of precisely stitched rectangles that carries the Maison’s identity into every surface the body touches.

The exterior tells a dual story: in Composition I, silver metallic dominates with orange accents. In Composition II, canyon orange takes the entire body. The Sport Silhouette keeps the G-Class sculpted — muscular, defined, present.

Two interior worlds: Expression I bathes everything in deep amber, monochrome and immersive. Expression II inverts: white leather with crystal resin accents glowing like amber lanterns against a gallery-white canvas.

Light, held in solid form. The art of Edo Kiriko, expressed in a car.

Target Connoisseur

The confident creative. Architects, gallerists, creative directors, horologists. They collect timepieces for the movement, not the brand. They understand materials at a molecular level — the difference between injection-moulded and hand-cast. Age 30–50. They drive to be seen — but by the right people.

Cadence

Internal: Timeless Almanac · HOF Sport Silhouette · Sculpted Surface

The Story

Cadence — from the Latin “cadentia,” the rhythm of falling tones. In music, a cadence is the moment of resolution — the chord that tells you where the composition arrives. The HOF Cadence arrives in two tones.

This is the Maybach principle, reimagined through the lens of Car Couture. The two-tone tradition — where a car’s silhouette is divided into two deliberate colour zones — has defined the most distinguished motorcars since the 1930s. Cadence takes this heritage and strips it to its essence: Schwarzwald Green below, Champagne above. The forest meets the salon.

The dividing line runs at the beltline — the classical proportion where the glass meets the body. Above: warm champagne that catches light like vintage cloth. Below: a deep, living green that carries the weight of the Black Forest itself.

Inside, the discipline continues. A single dark palette — black smooth leather, structured grain textures, hexagonal seat patterns, tonal stitching with deep emerald accents. Blue ambient lighting creates an atmosphere that feels less like a cabin and more like a private study at midnight.

The second composition offers the inverse mood: all dark green, a monochrome declaration where the two-tone heritage dissolves into a single, powerful conviction.

Two tones. One resolution.

Target Connoisseur

The architectural mind. They appreciate proportion, restraint, and the tension between two complementary tones. They collect watches (Patek Philippe, not Rolex), live in spaces designed by John Pawson, and see beauty in structural integrity. Age 40–60. Old-money sensibility with new-world awareness.

Seasonal Model · SS26

Ma

間 · Negative Space · HOF Salon Silhouette · Cleaned

The Story

Ma (間) — the Japanese concept of negative space. Not emptiness, but the interval that gives meaning to form. In architecture, Ma is the pause between walls that makes a room breathe. In music, it is the silence that makes the note matter. In a car, it is what remains after everything unnecessary has been removed.

Silver. One colour. One conviction. The all-silver exterior is not a choice — it is the absence of choice. The Salon Silhouette, cleaned of bumpers, becomes a pure geometric volume: a silver monolith that carries the philosophy of Bauhaus into motion.

Inside, two materials define the experience. The first is a high-pile bouclé woven textile — warm, tactile, enveloping. It covers the seats, the dashboard, and the upper cabin surfaces in a tone that feels like stepping into a purist living room. This is not automotive fabric. This is the textile of a Cassina sofa, of a Minotti armchair — a woven warmth that transforms the cabin into a space you want to inhabit, not just occupy.

The second is a metallic leather — a material that appears to be brushed silver metal but is, in fact, hand-finished leather. It creates a seamless dialogue between the silver exterior and the interior world: outside and inside speak the same material language. The metallic leather appears on seat bolsters, the centre console, and accent panels, dissolving the boundary between machine and room.

Between these two materials, the H-pattern emerges in decorative trim elements — HOF’s geometric signature, subtle, placed only where it earns its presence.

The Japanese tradition of ori (織) — the art of weaving — runs through Ma like a thread. From the Nishijin looms of Kyoto to the Bauhaus textile workshops of Dessau, the act of weaving is an act of patience: one thread at a time, one row at a time, until the whole emerges from the rhythm of making.

Less is not less. Less is everything that remains after everything unnecessary is removed.

Target Connoisseur

The minimalist. Their home is more gallery than mansion. They own one perfect watch, one perfect chair, one perfect knife. They read Kinfolk. They visit Vitsoe showrooms. They understand the difference between cheap simplicity and expensive reduction. Age 35–55. Design professionals, tech founders, quiet wealth. They don’t want to be noticed — they want to be right.

Brand Page

Inspiration — Car Couture

Why HOF exists. Where craftsmanship meets couture. From idea to expression.

The Page Story

This page is HOF’s manifesto in visual form. It answers one question: Why does this Maison exist?

The answer: Because the automobile deserves the same creative intelligence that fashion, art, and architecture have enjoyed for centuries. HOF brings together what belongs together — the engineering precision of automotive manufacturing with the creative conviction of haute couture.

The page flows through HOF’s creative process: Inspiration (where ideas begin — in culture, in art, in material) → Curation (how HOF selects, edits, and composes) → Collaboration (working with artisans, textile houses, cultural figures) → Craft (the atelier, the hand, the finish) → Expression (the final composition, on the road).
